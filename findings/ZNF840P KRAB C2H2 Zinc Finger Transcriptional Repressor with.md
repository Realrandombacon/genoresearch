## Description
ZNF840P (C20orf157) is a 716-amino acid nuclear protein functioning as a sequence-specific DNA-binding transcriptional repressor. Structural analysis reveals a canonical KRAB zinc finger architecture: N-terminal KRAB domain (IPR001909, PF01352, residues 42-81) serving as a protein-protein interaction module for recruiting co-repressors, followed by an extensive array of C2H2-type zinc finger domains (IPR013087, PF00096, PF13912) distributed throughout the protein (residues 151-173, 207-229, 235-257, 333-355, 389-411, and continuing through residue 716), each finger recognizing 3-4 base pairs of DNA major groove. The AlphaFold predicted structure (A6NDX5) shows medium confidence (pLDDT 68.3), consistent with multi-domain zinc finger proteins where individual fingers are well-structured but linker regions show flexibility. Expression data from HPA is unavailable, but KRAB-ZFPs typically show tissue-restricted expression patterns and nuclear localization. Protein interaction data from STRING is unavailable, though canonical KRAB domain partners include KAP1/TRIM28, SETDB1 histone methyltransferase, and HP1 heterochromatin proteins. ClinVar documents 9 pathogenic or likely pathogenic variants including large copy number losses (20q13.12-13.13) and deletions spanning the locus, indicating dosage sensitivity and potential disease association. The mechanistic hypothesis proposes that ZNF840P functions as a sequence-specific transcriptional silencer by binding genomic DNA through its zinc finger array and recruiting the KAP1 corepressor complex via its KRAB domain, leading to histone H3 lysine 9 trimethylation (H3K9me3) and heterochromatin formation at target loci. This repressive activity may regulate developmental genes, endogenous retroelements, or imprinted loci. The high variant burden suggests that ZNF840P dosage is critical for normal development, potentially through dysregulation of target gene networks or retrotransposon silencing.
