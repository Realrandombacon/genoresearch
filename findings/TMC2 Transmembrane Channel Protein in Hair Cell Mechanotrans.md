# TMC2: Transmembrane Channel Protein in Hair Cell Mechanotransduction Complex with Deafness Gene Interaction Partners

**Date:** 2026-03-18T03:59:53.919377

**Quality Score:** 10/10 (EXCELLENT)

## Description
TMC2 (C20orf145) is a 906-amino acid multi-pass transmembrane protein functioning as a putative ion channel subunit in the mechanotransduction complex of sensory hair cells. Structural analysis reveals a conserved TMC domain (IPR012496, PF07810, residues 574-689) embedded within a larger transmembrane channel-like fold (IPR038900, residues 66-827) spanning most of the protein sequence, characteristic of pore-forming membrane channels. The AlphaFold predicted structure (Q8TDI7) shows medium confidence (pLDDT 67.3), consistent with multi-pass transmembrane proteins where hydrophobic helices are well-predicted but extracellular/intracellular loops show flexibility. Expression profiling from the Human Protein Atlas demonstrates tissue-enhanced expression with highest levels in brain (2.7 nTPM) and plasma membrane localization, supporting a neuronal/sensory function. Protein interaction networks (STRING) reveal striking enrichment for deafness-associated genes and mechanotransduction complex components: LHFPL5 (score 0.994, LHFP-like protein 5), TMIE (0.932, transmembrane inner ear protein), TMC1 (0.893, transmembrane channel-like 1, causes human deafness DFNA36/DFNB7), PCDH15 (0.873, protocadherin-15, Usher syndrome type 1F), CIB2 (0.872, calcium/integrin-binding protein 2, causes deafness DFNB48), MYO7A (0.767, myosin-VIIa, Usher syndrome type 1B), and CDH23 (0.671, cadherin-23, Usher syndrome type 1D). This interaction profile places TMC2 within the tip-link mechanotransduction apparatus of cochlear and vestibular hair cells. ClinVar documents 25 pathogenic or likely pathogenic variants including copy number gains/losses on chromosome 20p13 and splice/nonsynonymous SNVs, indicating dosage sensitivity and disease association. The mechanistic hypothesis proposes that TMC2 forms a heteromeric complex with TMC1 and auxiliary subunits (TMIE, LHFPL5) to constitute the mechanically-gated ion channel responsible for converting sound-induced stereocilia deflection into receptor potentials. Functional redundancy or specialization with TMC1 may explain why TMC2 variants have not been definitively linked to monogenic deafness despite strong interaction evidence, though compound effects or vestibular-specific phenotypes remain possible.
