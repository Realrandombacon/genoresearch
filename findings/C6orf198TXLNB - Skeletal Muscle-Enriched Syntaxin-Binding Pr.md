# C6orf198/TXLNB - Skeletal Muscle-Enriched Syntaxin-Binding Protein in Vesicle Trafficking Network

## Description
C6orf198 encodes TXLNB (Beta-taxilin, UniProt Q8N3L3), a 684 aa intracellular protein involved in vesicle trafficking and syntaxin binding. InterPro identifies a taxilin family domain (IPR026183, PTHR16127, positions 7-498) with syntaxin binding function (GO:0019905), and a myosin-like coiled-coil domain (PF09728, positions 143-450) mediating protein-protein interactions. AlphaFold predicts a medium-confidence structure (pLDDT 67.1), suggesting a partially folded protein with ordered coiled-coil regions and potentially disordered segments. HPA classifies TXLNB as a predicted intracellular protein with group-enriched expression in skeletal muscle (280.9 nTPM), tongue (176.6 nTPM), and heart muscle (76.7 nTPM), with cancer-enhanced and immune cell-enhanced expression patterns. STRING interactions reveal high-confidence associations with vesicle trafficking machinery: TXLNA (0.791, alpha-taxilin), STX4 (0.783, syntaxin-4), STX7 (0.723, syntaxin-7), STX3 (0.707, syntaxin-3), TXLNG (0.648, gamma-taxilin), DTNBP1 (0.642, dystrobrevin-binding protein), STX8 (0.637, syntaxin-8), STX1A (0.582, syntaxin-1A), C1orf122 (0.683), and TMEM236 (0.627). This interaction network places TXLNB within the SNARE complex machinery regulating vesicle docking and fusion. ClinVar documents 14 pathogenic/likely pathogenic variants, predominantly large copy number losses and gains spanning 6q23-25 regions, though no specific disease phenotypes are established. Mechanistic hypothesis: TXLNB functions as a syntaxin-binding adaptor protein that regulates SNARE complex assembly during vesicle trafficking. The taxilin family domain mediates direct binding to Q-SNARE proteins (syntaxins), potentially competing with or facilitating SNARE complex formation. The coiled-coil domain enables oligomerization or scaffolding functions. Skeletal muscle enrichment suggests specialized roles in neuromuscular junction vesicle release, GLUT4 translocation, or sarcolemma repair. Despite domain characterization and interaction data, the specific vesicle populations regulated by TXLNB, its role in motor nerve regeneration, and disease mechanisms remain incompletely defined - TXLNB represents a 'partially dark' gene transitioning from annotation to functional understanding.
