# DIDO1: Large Nuclear PHD-SPOC Protein Regulating Apoptosis and Transcription via Chromatin Interactions

**Date:** 2026-03-18T04:07:15.829838

**Quality Score:** 9/10 (EXCELLENT)

## Description
DIDO1 (C20orf158, Death-inducer obliterator 1) is a 2240-amino acid nuclear protein functioning as a chromatin-associated transcriptional regulator and apoptosis modulator. Structural analysis reveals modular domain architecture: N-terminal PHD-type zinc finger (IPR001965, PF00628, residues 266-320) for histone tail recognition and chromatin binding, central transcription elongation factor S-II domain (IPR003618, residues 667-790) implicated in RNA polymerase II processivity, and C-terminal SPOC domain (IPR012921, residues 1049-1196) mediating transcriptional repression through interaction with RNA processing machinery. The AlphaFold predicted structure (Q9BTC0) shows low confidence (pLDDT 46.1), indicating extensive intrinsic disorder characteristic of large transcriptional co-regulators that function through flexible protein-protein interactions rather than rigid enzymatic scaffolds. Expression profiling from the Human Protein Atlas demonstrates ubiquitous expression across all tissues with low tissue specificity, consistent with a housekeeping role in fundamental nuclear processes, with subcellular localization to nucleoplasm, vesicles, and cytosol. Protein interaction networks (STRING) reveal striking enrichment for core histone H3 variants: H3-4 (score 0.980), H3C12 (0.967), H3-5 (0.963), H3-2 (0.963), H3C13 (0.963), H3C1 (0.945), H3C8 (0.936), H3C14 (0.933), H3-3A (0.932), and H3-3B (0.810)—placing DIDO1 directly within nucleosome complexes and chromatin regulatory machinery. ClinVar documents 37 pathogenic or likely pathogenic variants including large copy number losses (20q13.33), copy number gains (20q13.13-13.33), deletions, and splice/nonsynonymous SNVs, indicating strong dosage sensitivity and disease association. The mechanistic hypothesis proposes that DIDO1 functions as a chromatin reader/effector coupling histone modification state (via PHD finger binding to H3K4me3 or related marks) to transcriptional elongation control (via S-II domain) and co-transcriptional RNA processing (via SPOC domain). The apoptosis annotation suggests DIDO1 may integrate cell death signaling with transcriptional programs, potentially through cleavage-dependent activation or nucleocytoplasmic shuttling. High variant burden and histone interaction profile warrant investigation for roles in developmental disorders, cancer, or hematologic malignancies.
