# ADAM10: Ubiquitous Membrane-Anchored Metalloprotease Regulating Notch Signaling and Amyloid Precursor Protein Processing

## Description
ADAM10 (ADAM metallopeptidase domain 10, CD156c) is a 748-amino acid type I transmembrane protein functioning as a constitutive cell-surface metalloprotease and sheddase with critical roles in developmental signaling and neurodegeneration. Structural analysis reveals canonical ADAM domain architecture: N-terminal propeptide (residues 52-121) maintaining zymogen latency, catalytic metalloprotease domain (IPR001590, IPR034025, PF13574, residues 220-459) containing the conserved HEXGHNLGXXH zinc-binding motif for substrate cleavage, disintegrin domain (IPR001762, IPR036436, PF00200, residues 457-551) mediating integrin binding and cell adhesion, cysteine-rich domain (IPR049038, residues 591-646) involved in substrate recognition, and EGF-like domain (residues 649-681). The AlphaFold predicted structure (O14672) shows high confidence (pLDDT 80.1), confirming a well-folded extracellular protease module with stable transmembrane anchoring. Expression profiling from the Human Protein Atlas demonstrates ubiquitous expression across all tissues with low tissue specificity, consistent with essential housekeeping roles in fundamental signaling pathways, with subcellular localization to plasma membrane and vesicles—supporting dual functions in constitutive surface shedding and regulated intraluminal vesicle processing. Protein interaction networks (STRING) reveal striking enrichment for tetraspanin co-factors: TSPAN33 (score 0.987), TSPAN14 (0.977), TSPAN5 (0.973), TSPAN15 (0.952)—forming the ADAM10 tetraspanin web that regulates substrate specificity and surface trafficking—alongside DLG1 (0.982, postsynaptic scaffold), ADAM17 (0.958, related sheddase), APP (0.950, amyloid precursor protein), NOTCH1 (0.924, Notch receptor), HBEGF (0.453, heparin-binding EGF-like growth factor), and EFNA2 (0.419, ephrin-A2). These interactions place ADAM10 at the center of neurodevelopmental signaling (Notch cleavage), amyloid metabolism (alpha-secretase activity preventing Aβ formation), and growth factor regulation (EGF ligand shedding). ClinVar documents 21 pathogenic or likely pathogenic variants including large copy number losses (15q21.3-22.31), copy number gains, duplications, and splice variants (c.1696-115T>G), indicating dosage sensitivity and disease association. The mechanistic hypothesis proposes that ADAM10 functions as the primary alpha-secretase cleaving APP within the Aβ domain to prevent amyloid plaque formation, while simultaneously activating Notch receptors through S2 cleavage to regulate neurogenesis, synaptic plasticity, and cell fate decisions. Tetraspanin partners (TSPAN5/14/15/33) form membrane microdomains that concentrate ADAM10 with specific substrates, explaining substrate selectivity despite broad catalytic potential. Dysregulated ADAM10 activity contributes to Alzheimer disease pathogenesis (reduced alpha-secretase activity increases Aβ burden), cancer progression (enhanced growth factor shedding), and developmental disorders (impaired Notch signaling). High variant burden and essential protein classification warrant investigation for neurodevelopmental phenotypes, early-onset dementia, or hematologic malignancies.
