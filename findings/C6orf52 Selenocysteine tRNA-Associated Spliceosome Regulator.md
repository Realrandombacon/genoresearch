# C6orf52: Selenocysteine tRNA-Associated Spliceosome Regulator with Disease-Linked Variants

## Description
C6orf52 encodes a 152 amino acid uncharacterized protein (UniProt Q5T4I8) belonging to the tRNA selenocysteine 1-associated protein family (InterPro: IPR040434, residues 1-148; IPR041085 C-terminal domain, residues 16-147; Pfam: PF17654, residues 16-147). The protein shows medium structural confidence (AlphaFold pLDDT: 57.7), suggesting a partially folded architecture with intrinsically disordered regions that may enable conformational flexibility for multi-protein complex assembly.\n\nProtein interaction profiling (STRING-DB) reveals 10 high-confidence partners (scores 0.775-0.842) exclusively from the spliceosome machinery: PRPF39 (0.842, pre-mRNA processing factor 39), SNRPC (0.823, snRNP polypeptide C), RBM25 (0.816, RNA-binding motif protein 25), SNRPA1 (0.803, snRNP polypeptide A1), U2AF2 (0.795, U2 small nuclear RNA auxiliary factor 2), SF1 (0.788, splicing factor 1), SNRPD1 (0.780), SF3B1 (0.780), SNRPE (0.779), and SNRPG (0.775). This exclusive spliceosome interaction signature strongly implicates C6orf52 in pre-mRNA splicing regulation.\n\nClinVar contains 27 pathogenic/likely pathogenic variants, predominantly copy number gains and losses spanning 6p25.3-22.3 regions. While no monogenic disease is definitively established, the variant burden suggests dosage sensitivity and potential contribution to chromosomal instability syndromes or developmental disorders.\n\nMechanistic hypothesis: C6orf52 functions as a molecular adaptor that bridges selenocysteine tRNA modification machinery with the core spliceosome. The selenocysteine tRNA-associated domain anchors C6orf52 to SECIS-binding protein complexes, while intrinsically disordered regions enable dynamic recruitment of U2 snRNP components (U2AF2, SF1, SF3B1) to nascent transcripts. This positions C6orf52 as a coordinator of co-transcriptional splicing fidelity, particularly for selenoprotein mRNAs that require precise SECIS element recognition. Pathogenic copy number variants likely disrupt stoichiometric balance of spliceosome assembly, causing widespread splicing defects.
