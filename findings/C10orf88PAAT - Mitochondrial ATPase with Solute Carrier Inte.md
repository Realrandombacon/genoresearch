# C10orf88/PAAT - Mitochondrial ATPase with Solute Carrier Interaction Network and 51 ClinVar Variants

## Description
C10orf88 (Gene ID: 80007), also known as PAAT (ATPase PAAT), encodes a 445 amino acid mitochondrial hydrolase (UniProt Q9H8K7) located on chromosome 10q26.13. Originally annotated as a hypothetical open reading frame, PAAT is now recognized as a functional ATPase with defined enzymatic activity and protein interaction partners. InterPro analysis identifies a defining ATPase PAAT-like domain spanning the entire protein (IPR028043, residues 1-445) with a conserved Pfam match (PF14958, residues 59-198), indicating a structured catalytic core embedded within a larger regulatory scaffold. AlphaFold prediction reveals medium-confidence structure (pLDDT 65.8), suggesting the protein possesses a defined fold with flexible termini or linker regions typical of mitochondrial membrane-associated enzymes. Human Protein Atlas shows ubiquitous expression across all tissues with low tissue specificity, consistent with a housekeeping metabolic function. Subcellular localization is mitochondrial, aligning with its ATP hydrolysis activity and role in cellular energy metabolism. STRING interaction network reveals striking associations with solute carrier transporters: SLC38A4 (0.825, sodium-coupled neutral amino acid transporter), SLC7A8 (0.615, L-type amino acid transporter), SLC7A1 (0.598, cationic amino acid transporter), SLC1A5 (0.586, neutral amino acid transporter), SLC1A4 (0.528), SLC1A1 (0.526), and SLC2A3 (0.449, glucose transporter). These interactions position PAAT at the intersection of mitochondrial ATP hydrolysis and amino acid/carbohydrate transport regulation. Additional partners include the sodium channel SCN11A (0.825), the Polycomb gene SFMBT2 (0.718), and the angiogenesis factor SMOC1 (0.606). ClinVar documents 51 pathogenic/likely pathogenic variants, predominantly large copy number losses and gains spanning 10q26, indicating dosage sensitivity though no specific monogenic disease is definitively assigned. Mechanistically, PAAT likely functions as a mitochondrial ATPase that hydrolyzes ATP to drive conformational changes in associated solute carriers, facilitating amino acid and glucose uptake into mitochondria for oxidative phosphorylation. The interaction with multiple SLC transporters suggests PAAT may serve as a metabolic checkpoint, coupling ATP hydrolysis to substrate availability. Loss of PAAT function could impair mitochondrial metabolite transport, leading to bioenergetic defects and potentially contributing to metabolic disorders, neuromuscular disease (via SCN11A interaction), or developmental anomalies (via SFMBT2 chromatin regulation). The ubiquitous expression pattern and conserved ATPase domain indicate PAAT is essential for basal cellular metabolism across all tissue types.
