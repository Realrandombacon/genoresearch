# ADAM33: Membrane-Anchored Metalloprotease with Disintegrin Domain Implicated in Airway Remodeling and Asthma Pathogenesis

**Date:** 2026-03-18T04:02:57.610141

**Quality Score:** 10/10 (EXCELLENT)

## Description
ADAM33 (C20orf153) is a 813-amino acid transmembrane protein functioning as a zinc-dependent metalloprotease and cell adhesion molecule. Structural analysis reveals a modular domain architecture: N-terminal propeptide (PF01562, residues 52-121) regulating zymogen activation, catalytic metalloprotease domain (IPR001590, PF01421, residues 210-409) with conserved HEXGHNLGXXH zinc-binding motif, disintegrin domain (IPR001762, PF00200, residues 417-503) mediating integrin binding, cysteine-rich domain (IPR006586, residues 502-645) involved in substrate recognition and cell adhesion, and C-terminal EGF-like domain (IPR000742, residues 649-681). The AlphaFold predicted structure (Q9BZ11) shows high confidence (pLDDT 75.3), confirming a well-folded extracellular protease module. Expression profiling from the Human Protein Atlas demonstrates tissue-enhanced expression with highest levels in cervix (121.2 nTPM), along with detection in lung, fallopian tube, and esophagus, with subcellular localization to plasma membrane and nuclear speckles—suggesting potential dual roles in extracellular matrix remodeling and nuclear signaling. Protein interaction networks (STRING) identify PHF11 (score 0.905), a transcription factor linked to asthma susceptibility, as the top partner, alongside DPP10 (0.787), NPSR1 (0.698), IL4R (0.645), and ORMDL3 (0.640)—all genes associated with asthma, allergic inflammation, and airway hyperresponsiveness. This places ADAM33 within a genetic network controlling airway remodeling and immune dysregulation. ClinVar documents 30 pathogenic or likely pathogenic variants including large copy number gains (20p13-11.21) and deletions, indicating dosage sensitivity. The mechanistic hypothesis proposes that ADAM33 sheds ectodomains of growth factors, cytokines, or adhesion molecules (e.g., TNFα, EGFR ligands) to modulate inflammatory signaling and smooth muscle proliferation in airways. Its disintegrin domain may also directly regulate integrin-mediated cell-matrix interactions during tissue repair. Dysregulated ADAM33 activity likely contributes to asthma pathogenesis through excessive proteolysis, airway smooth muscle hyperplasia, and subepithelial fibrosis—consistent with its established role as a susceptibility gene for asthma and bronchial hyperresponsiveness.
