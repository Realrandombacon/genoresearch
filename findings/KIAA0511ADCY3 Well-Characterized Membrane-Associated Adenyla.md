## Description
KIAA0511 (now designated ADCY3, adenylate cyclase type 3) is a 1144-amino acid integral membrane protein functioning as a calmodulin-regulated class-III adenylate cyclase that catalyzes conversion of ATP to cyclic AMP (cAMP), serving as a critical second messenger generator for olfactory signal transduction and metabolic regulation. Structural analysis reveals canonical transmembrane adenylate cyclase architecture: N-terminal extracellular domain (IPR032628, PF16214, residues 42-297), two catalytic cyclase homology domains (cd07302, IPR001054, PF00211, residues 317-472 and 921-1119) containing the conserved catalytic site (IPR018297, residues 423-446 and 1052-1075) with metal-binding motifs for ATP coordination, and multiple transmembrane helices anchoring the enzyme to plasma membrane and primary cilium. The AlphaFold predicted structure (O60266) shows high confidence (pLDDT 76.4), confirming a well-folded catalytic core with stable membrane topology. Expression profiling from the Human Protein Atlas demonstrates ubiquitous expression across all tissues with low tissue specificity, consistent with essential housekeeping roles in cAMP signaling, with subcellular localization to plasma membrane, primary cilium, nucleoplasm, nucleoli, and Golgi apparatus—supporting compartmentalized cAMP production for distinct signaling pools. Protein interaction networks (STRING) reveal striking enrichment for cAMP signaling machinery: GNAL (score 0.975, olfactory G-protein alpha subunit), PRKACG (0.946, cAMP-dependent protein kinase catalytic gamma), CALM3/CALML4/CALML3/CALML5 (0.943-0.945, calmodulin calcium sensors regulating ADCY3 activity), PRKACA/PRKACB (0.824-0.827, PKA catalytic subunits), GNAS (0.814, stimulatory G-protein alpha), and RGS2 (0.485, GTPase accelerator). These interactions place ADCY3 within the canonical GPCR-cAMP-PKA signaling cascade. ClinVar documents 61 pathogenic or likely pathogenic variants including missense SNVs and copy number alterations, with strong disease association to obesity and metabolic syndrome. The mechanistic hypothesis proposes that ADCY3 functions as a calmodulin-dependent cAMP generator activated by calcium influx and G-protein signaling, producing compartmentalized cAMP pools that activate PKA holoenzymes to phosphorylate downstream targets regulating olfactory neuron activation, adipocyte metabolism, and insulin sensitivity. Primary cilium localization suggests roles in Hedgehog and Wnt signaling modulation via cAMP. Loss-of-function variants impair cAMP production, contributing to obesity pathogenesis through disrupted energy homeostasis and satiety signaling.
