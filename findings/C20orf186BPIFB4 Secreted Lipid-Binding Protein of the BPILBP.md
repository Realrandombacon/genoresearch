## Description
C20orf186 (now designated BPIFB4, Bactericidal Permeability-Increasing Protein Fold-Containing Family B Member 4, also known as LPLUNC4) is a 614-amino acid secreted protein functioning as a lipid-binding molecule with putative roles in innate immunity, microbial sensing, and inflammatory modulation. Structural analysis reveals a canonical BPI fold architecture consisting of two homologous globular domains: an N-terminal domain (IPR017942, SM00328, PF01273, residues 169-389) and a C-terminal domain (IPR001124, SM00329, PF02886, residues 412-614), both adopting the characteristic "boomerang" or "dumbbell" shape typical of the BPI/LBP/CETP superfamily (IPR017943, G3DSA:3.15.10.10/3.15.20.10). These domains are connected by a flexible linker region and mediate binding to lipopolysaccharides (LPS) and other amphipathic lipids. The AlphaFold predicted structure (P59827) shows medium confidence (pLDDT 68.4), reflecting the presence of well-folded globular domains interspersed with flexible loops, consistent with the dynamic nature of lipid-binding proteins that must accommodate diverse hydrophobic ligands. Expression profiling from the Human Protein Atlas demonstrates tissue-enriched expression with top expression in the pituitary gland (7.5 nTPM) and detection in single tissues, suggesting a specialized rather than ubiquitous role, potentially linked to neuroendocrine-immune crosstalk. Subcellular localization includes actin filaments and cytosol, though the N-terminal signal peptide predicts secretion into extracellular fluids. Protein interaction networks (STRING) reveal strong homology-based associations with other BPI family members: BPIFA3 (score 0.757), BPIFB1 (0.694), BPIFA2 (0.678), BPIFB6 (0.610), BPIFA1 (0.515), and BPIFB2 (0.403), as well as secretory globulins like SCGB1C1 (0.541) and lipocalins like LCN15 (0.445). These interactions place BPIFB4 within the mucosal innate defense system, likely functioning in parallel or redundancy with its paralogs. ClinVar documents 16 pathogenic or likely pathogenic variants, predominantly large copy number losses (20q11.21-13.12) and gains (20p11.21-q11.22), indicating genomic instability in this region and potential dosage sensitivity, though specific monogenic disease phenotypes remain to be fully elucidated. The mechanistic hypothesis proposes that BPIFB4 functions as an extracellular pattern recognition receptor or lipid chaperone, binding bacterial LPS or host-derived lipid mediators to neutralize endotoxin activity, modulate Toll-like receptor (TLR) signaling, or regulate lipid transport in mucosal environments. Unlike the potent bactericidal activity of BPI (BPIFA1), BPIFB4 may have evolved distinct immunoregulatory functions, potentially acting as a decoy receptor or anti-inflammatory agent. Dysregulation may contribute to chronic inflammatory diseases, respiratory infections, or pituitary disorders. This gene exemplifies successful resolution from "C20orf" dark gene annotation to a characterized member of the innate immune BPI family.
