# TSHZ2 - Multi-Zinc Finger Homeodomain Transcription Factor with Immune Enhancement

**Date:** 2026-03-17T02:25:59.943554

**Quality Score:** 8/10 (EXCELLENT)

## Description
TSHZ2 (Teashirt Zinc Finger Homeobox 2, UniProt Q9NRE2) is a 1034 aa transcription factor containing a conserved homeodomain (IPR001356, positions 840-914) for DNA recognition and three C2H2-type zinc fingers (IPR013087, positions 215-239, 275-304, 380-404) for chromatin binding. The protein belongs to the teashirt family (IPR027008, positions 1-1033), orthologous to Drosophila teashirt developmental regulators. HPA data reveals low tissue specificity with expression detected across many tissues, but shows immune cell enhancement and dual nucleoplasm/Golgi apparatus localization, suggesting potential roles in both transcriptional regulation and secretory pathways. STRING-DB identifies 7 medium-confidence interactions (scores 0.416-0.493) with GLI1 (Hedgehog pathway transcription factor, 0.493), HOXA7 (homeobox protein, 0.455), CBY1 (chibby Wnt antagonist, 0.453), SMARCE1 (SWI/SNF chromatin remodeling subunit, 0.442), EXD1 (exonuclease, 0.438), ZFHX2 (zinc finger homeobox protein, 0.423), and LGALS4 (galectin-4, 0.416), linking TSHZ2 to developmental signaling (Hedgehog/Wnt), chromatin remodeling, and immune recognition. ClinVar reports 10 pathogenic/likely pathogenic variants, predominantly copy number gains and losses spanning 20q13.2-13.33, indicating dosage sensitivity. Mechanistically, TSHZ2 likely functions as a sequence-specific DNA-binding repressor that recruits chromatin modifiers (via SMARCE1 interaction) to silence target genes involved in cell differentiation and immune cell maturation. The homeodomain mediates promoter recognition while zinc fingers enable chromatin tethering, potentially regulating hematopoietic lineage specification or inflammatory responses. Cross-domain reasoning suggests TSHZ2 bridges developmental transcription programs (homeodomain family) with immune cell function (HPA immune enhancement), possibly acting as a checkpoint regulator in myeloid or lymphoid differentiation.
